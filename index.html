Investing for beginners: How to start

October 16, 2020 5 min read

Ready to pull up your socks and get your finances in order? Growing your money is one of the most complex yet important parts of personal finance. Today, we’ll explain how to invest as a beginner — including types of investments and where to find them.

Sometimes, the world of investing can seem like an exclusive club, where only members have access to the top-secret instruction manual. You want to get started, but you feel like someone should have sat you down and taught you about it and never did.

But investing is for beginners, too. And there are a growing number of services and tools out there to help you get started today.

From deciding your strategy to choosing your investments and platform, here’s a brief guide to investment for beginners.

Should you begin investing now?

Some say that investing is like gambling, and you shouldn’t participate unless you’re prepared to lose it all. Others will tell you to get started as early as possible to grow your fortunes. 

Who should we believe?

Here are the facts. The earlier you start investing, the more money you can accumulate over time through compound interest. That’s earning interest on your interest.

For example, if you receive 20% interest after a year (a generous return) on a $1,000 investment ($200) and then reinvest the full $1,200 at the same rate, the next year you’ll also be making interest on your interest — this process repeated over time results in exponential growth.

However, if you pick risky investments (like putting all your money into the hot new IPO your buddy told you about), you could end up losing everything. It’s always better to choose the slow and steady gains than the quick and easy ones.

So, should you start investing now? Definitely, but you should always remember that if there is a significant chance of losing money it’s not an investment.

The more secure your financial position is and the more savings you have, the less of a risk it is for you to invest.

Now before you get started, here are a couple of things you should do to prepare.

Before investing

  • Set up an emergency fund or a sum of money that can cover your living expenses for a few months if everything goes down the drain.
  • Think of your goals: investing almost always means putting your money away for a significant chunk of time with minimal access. If you’re saving for a short-term financial goal, like buying a house, you might want to consider alternatives — more on that later.
  • Research your options: This goes without saying, but make sure you know what you are investing in before you invest in it.

Investment for beginners: A guide

Although many things class as “investments,” investing typically refers to buying financial products. We can broadly split these into two very broad categories: high and low-risk investments.

One of the most important aspects to consider when deciding how to invest as a beginner is surveying the risk involved.

High-risk investment

When we say high-risk, we’re usually referring to investments that have the potential to get you a lot of money in the short term. While high return does not always equal high-risk, it’s a good idea to thoroughly vet any investment that promises large rewards. Often they have a larger degree of risk.

High-risk options include:

  • Stocks. While stocks and investment are almost interchangeable terms these days, choosing stocks requires a lot of research. Companies that are shooting up right now could come crashing down in a recession. This can make them risky as many factors are hard to account for.
  • Foreign exchange (forex) is buying foreign currency in the hope the exchange rate will make it more valuable in the future. This is an incredibly volatile market and hard to play as a beginner.
  • Collectibles like comic books, baseball cards, or art can be a great hobby if you enjoy doing it. However, these items rarely give much return in a normal lifetime and are usually only valuable to other collectors.

Low-risk investment

Inversely, investments that promise lower returns tend to be low risk. These are generally what beginners should invest in.  At any level, you should try to have most of your money in low-risk investments for a long period. Once you have a steady profit you can always consider playing around with riskier options.

Low-risk options include things like:

  • Index funds.  These funds contain a wide range of individual stocks to diversify your portfolio and reduce risk. They are usually run by professional fund managers You can buy into them through a broker like Fidelity.
  • Bonds. These work similarly to an IOU to the government or a company.  They will pay you a small percentage of interest at the end of the bond term.
  • Employer pension plans. Your employer invests your money into diversified funds (which contain a range of stocks, bonds, and more). As they are run through your own company, the risk is quite low.

Notice something? Lower risk investments tend to be well-diversified, meaning they include a range of different assets. This means that if one asset fails, the overall return is unlikely to be severely affected since other assets may still perform well.

Where should beginners invest?

In recent years, a range of stock investment apps like Robinhood have emerged making headlines for making trading accessible to everyone.

But simplicity doesn’t equal safety. Just because they’re easy to use, it doesn’t make stock trading the safest investment. You’ll want to look into something lower risk to make up the “backbone” of your investments. 

However, many of the low-risk options don’t cut it either. High-yield savings accounts and bonds both used to give you decent returns with great security. But an American today is lucky to get over 1% APR on either one.

Unfortunately, unless you’re getting a return of 2%, you’re not beating inflation and effectively losing money.
